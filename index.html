Ghost Kings

The heavens fear them, the ghosts revere them, and the mortals whisper their names in dread. Crimson Rain Sought Flower walks among ruins, his silver butterflies trailing behind. Black Water sinks ships in the dead of night, swallowing souls into the abyss. Green Ghost Qi Rong laughs in chaos, feasting on the fear of the living. And White No-Face lingers in the shadows, a specter of nightmares long past. Together, they are the calamities that shake the realms."

Crimson Rain Sought Flower

Also known as Hua Cheng, he's currently most notorious of the four calamities, many legends and tales regarding his background and identity circulating in the realms. He's feared by many gods and ghosts, but also possesses worshippers. He's the master of Ghost City and wields the legendary scimitar E-Ming.

Night-Touring Green Lantern

Also known as Green Ghost Qi Rong or simply Rong, he is the only one out of the four calamities that is a wrath has not reached Supreme rank. He's known to try and imitate the others, hanging corpses up in forests to copy Hua Cheng's crimson rain and eating humans to emulate He Xuan's vast ghost devouring.

Ship-Sinking Black Water

Also known as demon He Xuan, he is a powerful water demon that emerged from the Kiln by devouring all ghosts within. Despite reaching Supreme level, he rarely comes out to cause trouble and keeps a low-profile. Very few people have seen him before, therefore, he’s of little concern. His base is the Black Water Demon Lair, which is guarded by his giant skeleton fish.

White-Clothed Calamity

Also titled as White No-Face or Bai Wuxiang, he's the oldest and most powerful of the four calamities. He is primarily known for being a big influence in Xianle's fall, and it was believed that he was defeated by Jun Wu many centuries ago.

TGCF Adaptations

Novel

Like ink spilling across parchment, the novel first unfurled in 2017, woven by the hand of Mo Xiang Tong Xiu. It was a tale carved in longing—a story of a fallen god, wandering with only tattered robes and a smile too soft for the heavens. A ghost king, cloaked in red, watched from the shadows, waiting for eternity to align their stars. It was here, in pages steeped with sorrow and devotion, that the legend began.

Manhua

Then came the manhua, illustrated by STARember, where lines and colors danced together, bringing faces to the names whispered between readers. Xie Lian’s warmth, Hua Cheng’s unwavering gaze—each stroke carried emotion, each scene a frozen moment of fate. The panels told the story anew, letting eyes see what hearts had always known.

Donghua

When the animated adaptation arrived, it did not walk—it soared. Wind and fire, battle and quiet longing, all unfolded with a rhythm that only motion could grant. The red butterfly fluttered through silver rain, vows were spoken in hushed tones beneath lantern light, and in the depths of ruin, a god’s hand met the fingers of his faithful ghost. Here, the story was no longer simply told; it lived.

Audio Drama

Then there were the voices—the audio drama, where words were carried by breath alone. Characters spoke, not in print or frame, but in the music of language. Laughter, anguish, devotion—the echoes of Xie Lian’s kindness and Hua Cheng’s quiet devotion rang clear, as if spoken by the heavens themselves.
